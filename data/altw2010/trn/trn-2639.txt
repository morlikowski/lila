'''סילְוָאן''' (או בעברית '''כפר השילוח''') היא ערבי סמוך לעיר. בתי השכונה בנויים בצפיפות על מדרון תלול. 

בתקופה עתיקה הושתתה תעלת-שלחין מקדרון; התעלה, והבריכה בסוֹפה, נקראו על-שם פעולת השלחין -  '''שיִלוֹ‏חַ‏'''.  הכפר נוסד סמוך לבריכת השילוח, ששימשה כמאגר המים של ירושלים בתקופת בית המקדש הראשון ובית המקדש השני לאחר חפירת הניקבה ממי הגיחון בימי המלך חזקיהו ומכאן שמו. בבריכת השילוח נהגו לקיים את טקס שאיבת המים בחג הסוכות כדי לקיים את מנהג ניסוך המים לפני חורבן הבית. היוונית הפכה את '''שילוח''', ל'''סילואָה''', והוסיפה לו את הסיומת "אן".

בתקופה הישראלית הקדומה שימש המקום כבית הקברות העיקרי של ירושלים. ארכאולוגים גילו ובדקו עשרות מערות קבורה בצלע ההר, בהם נתגלו ממצאים חשובים, בעיקר מתקופת בית ראשון. ראו למשל קבר ...יהו אשר על הבית.

בדומה לקהיר, בו תופסים חסרי-בית מערות-קבורה להתגורר בהם, גם כאן נתפסו מערות רבות למגורים על ידי מוסלמים. לכן, בין השאר, אחרי מחקר ארכאולוגי במערה כזו, החוקרים אוטמים את המערה אחריהם.

בשנת עולים מתימן שנקראה "'''כפר השילוחַ'''‏" או '''כפר התימנים''', בהנהגתו הרוחנית של  הרב פרעות 1921, ובשנת 1929 (מאורעות תרפ"ט), התנפלו הערבים על הנשארים, פגעו בהם ושדדו את רכושם. התושבים עזבו את המקום בלחץ השלטון הבריטי בעת מאורעות תרצ"ח. בני הכפר, בהנהגתו של הרב יוסף מדמוני בנו של מייסד הכפר חזרו לכפר למספר שנים, אך בעקבות מאורעות 1939 נאלצו לעזוב לצמיתות.
בעקבות עזיבת היהודים נבזז רכושם והבתים נהרסו כמעט לחלוטין. אחד מרבני היישוב אף נרצח בעת ניסיון להציל ספר תורה שנותר במקום. 
בשנים האחרונות התיישבו באזור משפחות ישראליות לאחר שהקרקעות נקנו מחדש, תוך סכסוכים רבים עם שכניהם הערבים המתנגדים לנוכחות ישראלית באזור. כיום חודש היישוב העברי בכפר התימנים, בסמוך לשכונה היהודית החדשה מעלה הזיתים ולהתיישבות היהודית בעיר דוד .

'''Silwan,''' or "'''Kfar Shiloah,'''" (; ) is an Old City of Jerusalem, extending along the Kidron Valley and running alongside the eastern slopes of Jabal Al-Mukaber. 
==Geography==
Historically, Silwan was located on the eastern slope of the Kidron Valley, near the outlet of the Gihon Spring at the Pool of Siloam, opposite the City of David. The villagers took advantage of the arable land to grow vegetables for market in Jerusalem.<ref> Cyclopaedia of Biblical , Theological and Ecclesistical Literature, John McClintock, Harper and Brothers, 1889, p. 745</ref> Nineteenth century travelers describe it as verdant and cultivated,<ref>''Handbook to the Mediterranean: Its Cities, Coasts and Islands,'' Robert Lambert Playfair, John Murray, Albemarle Street, London, 1892, p. 70.</ref><ref>''Biblical Geography and History,'' Charles Foster Kent , 1911 , p. 219</ref> and perched on a steep,  slippery scarp cut into hillside.<ref> ''The Holy Land and the Bible: A Book of Scripture Illustrations'', Cunningham Geikie , 1888 , New York, James Pott & Co. Publishers p.558</ref> Today, the region is more arid, but Palestinian herders still graze their goats on the hillsides.

==History==
Silwan is mentioned by the Arab writer and traveller ´Othman ibn ´Affan for the poor of the city. Lower down than this, again, is Job´s Well (Bir Ayyub). It is said that on the Night of ´Arafat the water of  the holy well Zamzam, at Makkah, comes underground to the water of the Spring (of Siloam). The people hold a festival here on that evening."<ref>Muk., 171. Quoted in Guy le Strange: ''Palestine under the Moslem'', 1890, p. 221.</ref> 

Numerous rock cuttings, steps and caves show the site has a long history of habitation, also by hermits. <ref>[http://www.bible-history.com/isbe/S/SILOAM%3B+SILOAH%3B+SHELAH%3B+SHILOAH/ Bible Encyclopedia entry: Siloam] International Standard Bible Encyclopedia.</ref> Silwan residents say that the construction of the village originated with the arrival of the Rashidun Caliph, Umar ibn al-Khattab. According to local legend, the Greek proprietors of Jerusalem were impressed by the humble majesty of the Caliph as he entered on foot while his servant rode in on camel, and presented him with the key to the city.<ref>Jeffrey Yas.[http://www.jerusalemquarterly.org/details.php?cat=2&id=76 "(Re)designing the City of David: Landscape, Narrative and Archaeology in Silwan"]; ''Jerusalem Quarterly,'' Winter 2000, Issue 7</ref> The Caliph thereafter granted the wadi to "Khan Silowna," an agricultural community of cave dwellers living around the valley spring.<ref>Jeffrey Yas.[http://www.jerusalemquarterly.org/details.php?cat=2&id=76 "(Re)designing the City of David: Landscape, Narrative and Archaeology in Silwan"]; ''Jerusalem Quarterly,'' Winter 2000, Issue 7</ref>

In 1834, during a large-scale peasants' rebellion against Ibrahim Pasha,<ref>http://www.britannica.com/eb/article-242896/Jerusalem#320040.hook</ref> thousands of rebels infiltrated Jerusalem through ancient underground sewage channels leading to the village of Silwan. <ref>''Jerusalem in the 19th Century: The Old City'' Yehoshua Ben-Arieh, Part II, Chapter One: Ottoman Rule, pp. 90, 109, Yad Ben Zvi Institute & St. Martin's Press, New York, 1984</ref> 

A traveler to Palestine in 1883, T. Skinner, wrote that the olive groves near Silwan were a gathering place for Muslims on Fridays.  <ref>''Jerusalem in the 19th Century: The Old City'' Yehoshua Ben-Arieh, Part II, Chapter Two: The Muslim Community, p. 133, Yad Ben Zvi Institute & St. Martin's Press, New York, 1984</ref>  

===Yemenite Jewish settlement===
In 1882, a group of Jews arrived from Yemen. Initially, they lived in tents. Later, when the rainy season began, they  moved into the ancient burial caves on the east side of the valley.<ref> Messianism, Holiness, Charisma, and Community: The American-Swedish Colony in Jerusalem, 1881-1933, Yaakov Ariel and Ruth Kark , ''Church History,'' Vol. 65, No. 4 (Dec., 1996), p. 645</ref> In 1884, the Yemenites moved into new stone houses built for them on the eastern slope of the Kidron, north of the Arab village, by a charity called ''Ezrat Niddahim.'' This settlement was called Kfar Hashiloach or the Yemenite Village. Construction costs were kept low by using the Shiloach as a water source instead of digging cisterns. An 1891 photo shows the homes on an otherwise vacant stretch of hillside.<ref> Photo can be found in “Documents of the Dream, Pioneer Jewish photographers in the Land of Israel, 1890-1933,” Vivienne Silver-Brody, Magnes Press, JPS, Philadelphia,1988, p. 40 </ref> An early 20th century travel guide writes: In the “village of Silwan , east of Kidron … some of the fellah dwellings [are] old sepulchers hewn in the rocks. During late years a great extension of the village southward has sprung up, owing to the settlement here of a colony of poor Jews from Yemen, etc. many of whom have built homes on the steep hillside just above and east of Bir Eyyub,”<ref> ''Cook's Handbook for Palestine and Syria'' , 
Thomas Cook Ltd., 1907, p. 105</ref>

The Yemenite Jews left Silwan during the 1936–1939 Arab revolt in Palestine and Arabs moved into the vacated buildings. After the 1948 Arab-Israeli War, Silwan was annexed by the Hashemite Kingdom of Jordan.<ref>http://www.alt-arch.org/timeline.html</ref> It remained under the Jordanian rule until 1967, when Israel captured the Old City. Until then, the village had delegates in the Jerusalem City Council.

===Urban growth===
thumb|350px|A view of the Arab village of Silwan from [[Abu Tor, looking towards the Separation Wall near the Old City]]
In the twentieth century, Silwan grew northward towards Jerusalem, expanding from a small farming village into an urban neighborhood. Modern Arab Silwan encompasses old Silwan (generally to the south), the Yemenite village (to the north), and the once-vacant land between. Today Silwan follows the ridge of the southern peak of the Mount of Olives to the east of the Kidron Valley, from the ridge west of the Ophel up to the southern wall of the Temple Mount/Haram al-Sharif.

==New Jewish settlement==
On maps issued by the Israeli government and organizations, part of what Arabs and others consider to be Silwan is labeled City of David (Ir David in Hebrew). Since Israel gained control over East Jerusalem in 1967, Jewish organizations have sought to re-establish a Jewish presence in Silwan. In 1987, the Permanent Representative of Jordan to the United Nations wrote to the Secretary-General to inform him of Israeli settlement activity; his letter noted that an Israeli company had taken over two Arab houses in the neighborhood of al-Bustan after evicting their occupants, claiming the houses were its property.<ref>[http://domino.un.org/unispal.NSF/3822b5e39951876a85256b6e0058a478/44ddc2496c28e0af052567f400544c98!OpenDocument "Letter dated 16 October 1987 from the Permanent Representative of Jordan to the United Nations addressed to the Secretary-General"] UN General Assembly Security Council</ref> Settlement expansion has increased since 1991.<ref>[http://www.mathaba.net/0_index.shtml?x=591356 Elad in Silwan: Settlers, Archaeologists and Dispossession]</ref><ref>[http://apjp.org/silwan/ Architects and Planners for Justice in Palestine]</ref> Wadi Hilwe, an area of Silwan close to the western wall of the Old City, wherein lies the neighborhood of Al-Bustan, has been a focus of Jewish settlement.
