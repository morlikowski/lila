'''Bodyline''', also known as '''fast leg theory''', was a Ashes tour of batting skill of Australia's bowler deliberately aimed the legside deflections that could be caught by one of several fielders in the quadrant of the field behind square leg. 

Although several batsmen were hit during the series, as would be expected, no one was hit while a leg-theory field was set, but still it led to ill feeling between the two national teams, with the controversy eventually spilling into the diplomatic arena. Over the next two decades, several of the Laws of Cricket were changed to prevent this tactic being repeated. It should be noted, however, that the ''occasional'' short-pitched ball aimed at the batsman is not and has never been illegal and is in widespread use today as a tactic. Law 42 includes: ''The bowling of fast short pitched balls is dangerous and unfair if the umpire at the bowler's end considers that by their repetition and taking into account their length, height and direction they are likely to inflict physical injury on the striker...' <ref>[http://www.lords.org/laws-and-spirit/laws-of-cricket/laws/law-42-fair-and-unfair-play,68,AR.html Law 42 on fair and unfair play.] Retrieved 20 April 2008.</ref>

The inspiration for the idea probably came from the former England and Surrey captain Percy Fender who when seeing Newsreel film of Bradman batting against the MCC on the 1930 tour noticed that Bradman tended to jolt to the leg side when faced with short-pitched deliveries. Bradman was known rarely if ever to play the hook shot and Fender therefore felt Bradman might be vulnerable to fast, short-pitched deliveries on the line of leg stump.<ref> Douglas Jardine, In Quest of the Ashes, London, 1934</ref><ref name="p244">Pollard, p. 244.</ref>  

Accompanying this bowling line would be a cordon of close fielders set on the bat, possibly giving catching chances to the close-set leg side field.  A similar tactic, known as pitch outside leg stump to restrict a batsman's scoring opportunities.

Larwood and Voce practised the plan over the next two seasons of English county cricket, terrorising their opponents as Nottinghamshire finished near the top of the competition each year.<ref>Frith, pp. 44&ndash;45.</ref> By the time the English team left for Australia on September 17, 1932, Larwood and Voce, along with Bill Bowes from Yorkshire, had perfected their attack.<ref>John Arlott, ''Arlott on Cricket'', Fontana, 1985, p193 gives the departure date.</ref><ref>Jardine, Douglas. ==English tour 1932–33 ==

The English players first tried their tactic in a first-class tour match against an Australian XI in Melbourne on 18–22 November, a game in which Jardine rested and gave the captaincy duties to his deputy Bob Wyatt.<Ref name="p249">Pollard, p. 249.</ref> Seeing the bruising balls hit the Australian batsmen on several occasions in this game and the next angered the spectators.<ref>Frith, pp. 98, 106.</ref>

The English players and management were consistent in referring to their tactic as ''fast leg theory'' because most of them considered it to be a variant of the established — and relatively harmless — see below). The reporting of the series in England described the tactic as ''fast leg theory''. This caused serious misunderstandings, as neither the English public nor the Board of the Freddie Brown and the Nawab of Pataudi opposed it, as did Walter Hammond and Les Ames among the professionals.<ref>E.W. Swanton. ''Sort of a Cricket Person'', William Collins & Sons, 1972, p. 19.</ref>

Jardine however insisted his tactic was not designed to cause injury and that he was leading his team in a sportsmanlike and gentlemanly manner, arguing that it was up to the Australian batsmen to play their way out of trouble. He also secretly sent a telegram of sympathy to Bert Oldfield's wife and arranged for presents to be given to his young daughters,<ref>Frith, p. 201.</ref> a gesture open to a variety of interpretations.

The situation escalated into a diplomatic incident between the countries as the MCC — supported by the British public and still of the opinion that their ''fast leg theory'' tactic was harmless — took serious offence at being branded "unsportsmanlike" and demanded a retraction. With World War I still fresh in people's memories and the first rumblings of World War II beginning, many people saw Bodyline as fracturing an international relationship that needed to remain strong.<ref>Frith, pp. 241–259.</ref>

Jardine, and by extension the entire English team, threatened to withdraw from the fourth and fifth Tests unless the Australian Board withdrew the accusation of unsporting behaviour.<ref name="p259">Pollard, p. 259.</ref><ref>Frith, p. 222.</ref> Public reaction in both England and Australia was outrage directed at the other nation. The Governor of Alexander Hore-Ruthven, who was in England at the time, expressed his concern to British Secretary of State for Dominion Affairs James Henry Thomas that this would cause a significant impact on trade between the nations.<ref>Frith, pp. 242&ndash;248.</ref><ref name="p2601"/>

The standoff was settled only when Australian Prime Minister Joseph Lyons met with members of the Australian Board and outlined to them the severe economic hardships that could be caused in Australia if the British public boycotted Australian trade. Given this understanding, the Board withdrew the allegation of unsportsmanlike behaviour two days before the fourth Test, thus saving the tour.<ref>Frith, pp. 255–259.</ref><ref name="p2601">Pollard, pp. 260&ndash;261.</ref>

The English team continued to bowl Bodyline in the remaining two Tests, but slower pitches meant the Australians, although frequently bruised, sustained no further serious injuries.

'''Bodyline''' est le nom d'une tactique de équipe d'Australie lors des Ashes de 1932-33, disputées en Australie, en particulier pour contrer les talents de batteur de Donald Bradman.

Initiée par le capitaine anglais anglais, à viser délibérément le corps du batteur adverse avec leurs balles après le rebond de celles-ci. L'objectif était d'obliger les batteurs à protéger leur corps avec leurs battes, rendant les balles faciles à attraper au vol, après l'impact avec la batte, par un grand nombre de ''fielders'' anglais placés juste derrière le batteur. Une balle attrapée au vol par les joueurs adverses signifie en effet l'élimination du batteur.

Bien que plusieurs batteurs australiens furent blessés durant la série de matchs, aucun ne le fut alors que les ''fielders'' anglais étaient en position pour appliquer ''Bodyline'', mais cela instaura un malaise entre les deux équipes nationales, et la controverse se déplaça finalement sur le terrain règles du cricket furent modifiées pour éviter que cette tactique ne soit réutilisée. Il est à noter toutefois que les balles dirigées vers le corps du batteur ne sont pas et n'ont jamais été interdites et font encore partie actuellement de l'arsenal tactique des lanceurs.

=== Mise en place de la tactique ===
250px|right|thumb|Positions possibles des ''[[fielders'' sur le terrain, lorsque le batteur adverse est droitier. Le côté ''leg side'' du batteur est ici la partie droite du terrain.]]
left|100px|thumb|Pour ce batteur droitier, le ''[[stump|leg stump'' est le piquet à droite de la photo.]]
Après la série de 1930, l'international anglais Bradman avec l'ancien capitaine anglais et capitaine du leg stump'', c'est-à-dire le piquet du ''wicket'' situé du côté de la jambe du batteur, et de faire fuser la balle vers le corps du batteur. Les deux lanceurs affirment qu'ils en sont capables, et que cela peut s'avérer efficace<ref name="df"/>.

Pour accompagner ces lancers, la tactique inclut un grand nombre de ''fielders'' du côté ''leg side'' du batteur, c'est-à-dire derrière lui. Le résultat est que le batteur adverse a à choisir entre soit essayer d'éviter la balle dirigée vers son corps ou sa tête, soit tenter de l'écarter avec sa batte, donnant ainsi la possibilité aux ''fielders'' d'attraper la balle au vol et donc de l'éliminer. Une tactique similaire, appelée ''leg theory'', a déjà été utilisée précédemment par des ''slow bowlers'' tels que Fred Root, mais avec une disposition des joueurs de champ plus conventionnelle et des lancers moins rapides<ref>[http://content-uk.cricinfo.com/wisdenalmanack/content/story/151764.html Body-line methods, 1933] sur cricinfo.com</ref>

Larwood et Voce pratiquent la tactique durant les deux saisons du ''1932, Larwood, Voce et le joueur du Yorkshire County Cricket Club Bill Bowes se sont perfectionnés<ref>Douglas Jardine, ''In Quest for the Ashes''</ref>.
